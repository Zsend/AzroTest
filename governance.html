Governance

Policy, reporting, and decision integrity.

A treasury program fails more often from bad process than from bad markets. Governance is the product: written rules, clear approvals, and a reporting cadence that keeps everyone aligned.

Core principle

Stability is a feature.

The strongest treasury policies are boring: they remove discretion, establish guardrails, and rely on time.

Document decisions

Who can approve? What triggers changes? Where is the log?

Separate custody & approvals

Define access, multi‑sig roles, and operational security procedures.

Governance controls

Use the policy memo template to formalize these controls. The goal is to survive the worst weeks and still execute.

ControlPurpose
Treasury boundaryDefine what % of cash flow is eligible and what is not. Maintain minimum operating cash and tax reserves.
Buffer policySet a dedicated buffer (cash or near-cash) so the business is not forced to sell BTC during drawdowns.
Execution rulesDocument weekly cadence, authorized venues, and any signal-based intensity adjustments.
Custody & key managementChoose custody model (self-custody, multi-sig, institutional). Define who can move funds and under what approvals.
Trim policy (optional)If trims are used, define triggers, maximum trim size, and where proceeds go (buffer, capex, growth, dividends).
Reporting cadenceProduce a monthly report with holdings, cost basis, policy compliance, and material events.
Change managementRules change only after scheduled review and documented approval, not in response to price swings.

Monthly report template

Implementation note

Governance should match your size. A solo owner can still write a policy and keep a decision log; a multi‑partner shop may require additional approvals and multi‑sig structure.
