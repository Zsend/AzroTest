Sources & method

The numbers have to mean what they say.

Justice data are fragmented. We would rather publish an honest unknown than a confident number that cannot be defended.

Source-linkedDefinitions statedLimits visibleReviewed June 22, 2026

The current national count is unknown.

No single public system provides a reliable, current count of everyone incarcerated for cannabis across federal and state prisons, local jails, pretrial detention, tribal systems, juvenile facilities, immigration detention, secondary offenses, or supervision violations.

The often-repeated “40,000” figure was based loosely on older data. A later rough calculation suggested about 32,000 people in state and federal prisons, but even the organization explaining that estimate says it is not current or precise.

Our launch language: The exact number is unknown. The goal is zero.

What the federal chart shows.

The homepage chart uses U.S. Sentencing Commission data for federal marijuana trafficking cases sentenced in fiscal years 2021 through 2025: 995, 806, 561, 471, and 383.

It is not a count of people currently incarcerated. It covers federal sentencing only. It must not be described as a nonviolent-case count: the Commission reports that some cases received weapon-related sentence enhancements.

What we intend to measure.

Career outcomes should be counted only after they happen. As the network develops, the public ledger should distinguish:

  • real paid opportunities received;
  • qualified introductions made;
  • interviews, offers, and jobs started;
  • starting pay, hours, and benefits where participants consent to aggregate reporting;
  • 30-, 90-, 180-, and 365-day retention;
  • promotions, increased responsibility, compensation growth, supplier work, and ownership pathways.

We will not count a pledge as a hire, a course as a job, or a start date as retained work.
