Lesson 1

Your abilities can grow.

Growth mindset means you are not limited to who you are today. You can build skills when you practice, try better strategies, ask for feedback, and keep going through mistakes.

1 of 5

Fixed mindset

  • “I am bad at this.”
  • “Mistakes mean I should stop.”
  • “Needing help means I am not smart.”

Growth mindset

  • “I am not good at this yet.”
  • “Mistakes show me what to practice next.”
  • “Feedback helps me find a better strategy.”
Important: people are not simply born with a growth mindset. It can be developed over time, like building a muscle. Growth mindset is not magic, but it gives you a better way to respond when learning gets hard.

Watch for these ideas

  • Hard does not mean impossible. It means your brain has something to learn.
  • Mistakes are information. They point to the next practice step.
  • Better strategy matters. Effort works best when you improve how you practice.

Lesson 2

Build your starter ikigai.

Ikigai can mean a reason to get up in the morning. For kids, this is not a forever career choice. It is a smart direction to test: what gives you energy, who you want to help, what skill you want to grow first, and what small project you can try next.

2 of 5

Use this as a direction builder

  • Spark: What topics or activities make you curious?
  • Help: Who or what do you care about helping?
  • Skill: What useful ability should you grow first right now?
  • Experiment: What tiny project could teach you if this direction fits?
Important: a growth mindset means you can improve many things. Ikigai is not asking what you are permanently capable of. It helps you choose what to focus on and test first, because time and attention are limited.

Step 1: Choose your current sparks.

Pick one to three. These are interests to test, not forever labels.

Step 2: Choose who or what you want to help.

Pick the one that feels most meaningful right now.

Step 3: Choose how you like to create value.

This is not a limit. It is just your first focus for the next small experiment.

Lesson 3

Break problems down to what is true.

First principles thinking means you do not only copy what everyone else does. You slow down, find the true building blocks, question guesses, and build a better answer from the ground up.

3 of 5

Copy-first thinking

  • “Everyone does it this way, so I have to do it too.”
  • “This looks too hard, so there must be no way.”
  • “I will guess before I understand the pieces.”

First principles thinking

  • “What do I know is true?”
  • “What are the smallest pieces of this problem?”
  • “What tiny test can teach me what to do next?”
Kid version: first principles thinking is like taking apart a LEGO build, studying the blocks, and then building your own better version. It helps you solve problems instead of just copying answers.

Adult setup note: embedded video works best from a hosted HTTPS link such as GitHub Pages or Netlify.

Watch for these ideas

  • Question assumptions. Do not accept “that is just how it works” without thinking.
  • Find the building blocks. Break the problem into pieces you can understand.
  • Build from what is true. Then choose one small test to learn faster.

The 4-question method

  • Problem: What am I trying to solve or improve?
  • Facts: What do I know is actually true?
  • Assumptions: What might I be guessing or copying?
  • Small test: What can I try this week to learn faster?

Example

If math feels hard, do not just say “I am bad at math.” Break it down: Which type of problem? Which step goes wrong? What fact do I need? What five-minute practice test can I run today?

Lesson 4

Tiny improvements can become huge.

Now choose one small useful action from your direction and first principles plan. The magic is not one big day. The power comes from stacking many small days.

4 of 5

Adult setup note: embedded video works best from a hosted HTTPS link such as GitHub Pages or Netlify.

Watch for these ideas

  • Small steps count. A tiny useful action can look small today but matter over time.
  • Consistency beats one big burst. The goal is to keep returning to the next step.
  • Progress compounds. Each improvement gives you a slightly better starting point.

After 30 days

1.35x

About 35% better than where you started.

After 100 days

2.70x

More than double your starting point.

After 1 year

37.8x

The idea is 1.01 multiplied by itself 365 times.

Simple version: if you improve by 1% today, tomorrow you are improving from a slightly better starting point. Then the next day starts from that better point too. That is compounding. It is like building a tower one small brick at a time.
1.01 × 1.01 × 1.01 ... for 365 days ≈ 37.8 times

10 years

The pure math becomes about 5.9 quadrillion times. That is too huge to picture.

20 years

The pure math becomes astronomically larger. At that point, the exact number matters less than the lesson.

Real life

People have rest days, setbacks, and plateaus. The lesson is not “be perfect.” The lesson is “keep choosing the next useful step.”

Design a system that helps you grow.

Now turn your thinking into a repeatable setup. Systems thinking means looking at the parts, the connections, and the feedback loops. Instead of only saying “try harder,” you design a setup that makes the right action easier to repeat.
