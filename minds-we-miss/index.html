Home→The Minds We Miss
Designed for everyone. Built to reach the people systems miss.

Some minds are punished before they are understood.

Neurodivergence is not a side cause and it is not a test for who deserves help. It is a design lens that reveals where systems fail hardest—and how to build a baseline that works better for everyone.

The rule

No diagnosis is required to deserve the baseline.

No diagnosis excuses harm. Different minds still need systems capable of teaching, communicating, and supporting change.

Why this belongs in the mission

The justice system is a pressure test for every weakness in how society understands different minds.

Traits connected to attention, sensory processing, communication, executive function, learning differences, brain injury, trauma, or overload can be misread as laziness, defiance, manipulation, aggression, or refusal.

Under-identified

People may enter custody without diagnosis, accommodations, or language for what they experience.

Misinterpreted

Shutdown, overwhelm, impulsivity, blunt communication, or difficulty following complex instructions can be treated only as misconduct.

Unsupported under pressure

Isolation, uncertainty, sensory stress, disrupted treatment, and loss of agency can remove protective factors when people need them most.

The universal frame

People arrive through many doors.

Some made one destructive choice. Others were shaped by poverty, violence, addiction, unstable homes, disability, disrupted education, trauma, or opportunities that never reached them. Some caused real harm. Some were harmed long before they harmed anyone. Every one of them should have a real path to learn, stabilize, take responsibility, and build a different future.

The baseline belongs to everyone.

Support cannot depend on exceptional talent, innocence, a diagnosis, or whether the public finds a person easy to relate to.

Design for the hardest-to-reach user.

A system that works for people with low literacy, sensory stress, executive-function challenges, trauma, or unusual cognition will work better for everyone.

Potential under pressure

Difference can become contribution—or collapse.

A Right For All does not romanticize neurodivergence. It refuses to assume that a person who struggles in a conventional system has nothing valuable to contribute.

Build predictability.

Clear language, routines, visual structure, and low-friction navigation can reduce unnecessary cognitive load.

Build regulation support.

Grounding, planning, sensory awareness, and emotional vocabulary can help people respond before a crisis escalates.

Build useful challenge.

People with intensity, curiosity, pattern recognition, technical interest, creativity, or deep focus need somewhere constructive to direct it.

Build continuity.

Support that disappears at release leaves people to navigate employment, housing, treatment, relationships, and stigma alone.

Evidence with restraint

The risk is serious. The claims must stay honest.

Research finds elevated suicide risk among autistic people and serious vulnerability after release from prison. Inspectors have also reported that neurodivergent people in custody can remain unidentified, unsupported, and disadvantaged. The exact combined risk is not captured by one responsible multiplier, so A Right For All will not manufacture one.

Autism and suicide

AFSP summarizes evidence of elevated suicide risk and the importance of unmet support needs, burnout, masking, isolation, and marginalization.

Review AFSP source

Neurodiversity in prisons

HM Inspectorate of Prisons has described inconsistent identification, support, training, and reasonable adjustments.

Review inspectorate source

Overrepresentation

UK government materials have cited estimates that a substantial share of the adult prison population may be neurodivergent under broad definitions.

Review government source

Post-release suicide risk

Population studies identify the period after release as a high-risk time requiring continuity, connection, and support.

Review cohort-study summary

The Minds We Miss agenda

Research, design, support, and talent development—not labels.

Research

Connect fields that often remain separate: neurodiversity, corrections, suicide prevention, education, reentry, and human-centered technology.

Design

Build accessible learning and regulation tools with qualified experts and users.

Continuity

Support the transition from custody into work, care, family, and community.

Talent

Create proof-over-pedigree paths for unusual thinkers to contribute without diagnosis disclosure.
