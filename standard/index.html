The first public campaign

Make the free section a right.

Whenever a correctional system provides tablets, the free section should include real education, basic wellness, accessible design, and preparation for release.

See the standardHelp improve it
The public promise

No one should have to buy their way into a chance to change.

The baseline remains free to the person in custody and their family.

Free Floor Standard v0.1

Four parts. One minimum.

The standard is narrow on purpose: simple enough to understand, measure, require, and defend.

1

Free GED preparation

Learning and study resources built to work on correctional tablets and available without user or family charges.

2

Basic wellness

Simple, nonclinical tools for grounding, reflection, regulation, goals, routines, and daily structure.

3

Designed for different minds

Readable, predictable, low-friction access for different literacy, attention, sensory, communication, and planning needs.

4

Preparation and continuity

Build a release plan, carry goals and learning across transfers, and continue the path when custody ends.

What basic wellness means

Useful daily support without pretending to replace treatment.

The Free Floor can support grounding, breathing, routines, sleep habits, emotional vocabulary, planning, reflection, goal-setting, and knowing when to ask for help.

What it must avoid

  • Diagnosis or therapy claims
  • Advertising or paid upgrade prompts inside the baseline
  • Unnecessary data collection
  • Confusing navigation or inaccessible language
  • Any claim that software replaces human care
Why a right—not an upgrade

Custody does not erase every basic obligation.

Correctional systems already recognize that some forms of access cannot be reduced to paid extras. A Right For All is fighting to add a separate education-and-wellness baseline wherever tablets are used.

The principle

Essential access belongs in the free section.

Learning, basic daily support, and preparation for release should not depend on money in a person’s account or money from family.

The campaign

The Free Floor Act

A working policy concept to make the Free Floor a statutory and contractual requirement—not a voluntary feature or a proprietary paid upgrade.

The policy goal

Turn a moral claim into a required baseline.

A Right For All is working toward a new statutory and contractual standard—not claiming that current law already guarantees it.

Model requirement

Any correctional agency or facility that provides electronic tablets to people in custody shall make available, without charge to the user or family, a baseline package of GED preparation, foundational education, basic nonclinical wellness, accessibility features, and release-preparation resources.

In legislation and policy

Create the minimum requirement across facilities that use tablets.

In purchasing and contracts

Define what must be free, usable, private, accessible, supported, and maintained.

In public accountability

Measure whether the Free Floor is present, understandable, reliable, and genuinely free.

Vendor-neutral by design

Require the outcome—not one proprietary application.

The standard should work with open resources, licensed content, existing vendors, future providers, and different technical rails. That makes it easier to scale and harder to lock away.

Free at the point of use

No prisoner charge, family charge, advertisement, or paywall for the core baseline.

Accessible and offline-capable

Useful in restricted environments and for people with different literacy and cognitive needs.

Measurable

Facilities can show whether access exists and whether it actually works.

Help improve the standard

Build the first version with the people who know the system.

Educators, corrections professionals, disability experts, clinicians, privacy lawyers, purchasing specialists, technologists, researchers, and people with lived experience can define what is useful, safe, measurable, and realistic.
