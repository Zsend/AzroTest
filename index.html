Why this exists

The law changed. The record stayed.

Cannabis moved from contraband to commerce. Companies, careers, and wealth followed. Many people convicted under prohibition did not.

A record can close a door before anyone sees the work, the discipline, or the person who kept moving forward.

That costs the person. It can also cost the employer the contributor their normal hiring system never lets them meet.

A conviction should not earn someone a job. It should not erase everything they can do—or decide how far they can go.

For the industry

Open the industry. Keep the standards.

We are not asking companies to hire from guilt. We are asking them not to stop evaluating a qualified person after one line in a background report.

01Bring something real.

One paid role, project, apprenticeship, or mentorship relationship.

02Make the terms clear.

State the work, pay, requirements, location, timeline, and genuine legal restrictions.

03Review the person.

Consider current ability and role fit individually where the law allows.

04Leave room to grow.

Opportunity should reach beyond entry-level placement into responsibility, leadership, and ownership.

The larger goal

The count should be known. The goal should be zero.

America does not maintain a reliable, current national count of people incarcerated for cannabis. We will not turn uncertainty into a marketing statistic.
