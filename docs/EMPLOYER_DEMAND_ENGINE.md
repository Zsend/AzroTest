# Employer Demand Engine

## The constraint

A search for companies publicly advertising that they hire people with cannabis felonies is the wrong demand-generation strategy. Most employers will not use that language even when they are open to individualized hiring, and public statements do not create jobs.

Justice Grows must manufacture accountable demand: committed paid roles, named hiring managers, lawful review, candidate support, and measured retention.

## The offer

Justice Grows gives employers a lower-friction way to hire qualified justice-impacted talent:

- Candidate-owned skills profiles and human-reviewed shortlists
- Role and jurisdiction pathway review
- Fair-chance hiring workflow and manager training
- Federal Bonding Program coordination when applicable
- Document, transportation, training, relocation, and retention partners
- Candidate-consented introductions
- 30/90/180/365-day support
- Auditable impact reporting
- Public recognition earned by outcomes, not a pledge

The service does not conduct adverse background screening, promise legal eligibility, or replace employer counsel.

## Founding 50 × 2 campaign

Recruit 50 employers that each commit at least two approved roles per quarter. This creates a target of up to 400 openings a year before replacement hiring. It is a capacity target, not a placement claim.

### Minimum founding commitment

- Named executive sponsor and operating owner
- Signed Fair Chance Employer Compact
- At least two real roles in the first quarter
- Compensation range, duties, schedule, location, manager, and growth path
- No blanket conviction ban except where a documented legal requirement applies
- Candidate privacy and candidate-consent agreement
- Timely interview and disposition reporting
- 30/90/180/365-day outcome reporting
- Escalation channel for licensing, safety, wage, privacy, or discrimination concerns

## Employer segments

### 1. Regulated cannabis operators

Prioritize licensees with centralized HR, repeat hiring, multiple job families, and a credible executive sponsor. Evaluate employee badge, ownership, transport, security, and local rules separately. Do not infer an employee bar from an owner/license-applicant rule.

### 2. Ancillary cannabis businesses

Software, packaging, equipment, testing support, construction, HVAC, facilities, logistics, professional services, marketing, finance, compliance support, media, and training can provide industry participation without every role being plant-touching.

### 3. Vendors and suppliers to major operators

Use supplier standards and procurement leverage. Ask large operators to require or reward fair-chance practices among vendors, and to fund placements across their supply chain.

### 4. Remote and multi-state employers

Create location-independent pathways in customer success, sales development, administration, design, technology, bookkeeping, operations support, and content—subject to role-specific law and employer requirements.

### 5. Unions, apprenticeships, and community colleges

Construction, electrical, HVAC, warehousing, manufacturing, logistics, and technical credentials can create durable mobility inside or adjacent to the industry.

### 6. Social-equity and community-rooted licensees

Treat these employers as strategic partners, but do not assume they have spare hiring capacity. Subsidize onboarding, wage support, training, and HR infrastructure where appropriate.

## Enterprise sales motion

### Qualification

Score prospects on hiring volume, job-family fit, legal-market footprint, HR maturity, executive sponsorship, compensation quality, data-reporting willingness, and candidate-safety history.

### Sequence

1. Executive invitation: repair obligation plus workforce value
2. Twenty-minute readiness diagnostic
3. Application and compact
4. Policy/application review
5. First-role workshop
6. Hiring-manager training
7. Candidate shortlist and consented introductions
8. Interview support
9. Start and retention support
10. Outcome verification and public tier

### Close on a role, not a logo

An employer is not “live” until an actual role has passed review. Public coalition membership should show:

- **Pledged:** compact and executive sponsor
- **Verified:** policy review, manager training, and at least one approved role
- **Proven:** confirmed starts, current outcomes, candidate trust checks, and no unresolved material complaint

## Risk-reduction bundle

- Federal Bonding Program coordination where the worker and role qualify; the Department of Labor describes no-cost fidelity coverage for the first six months.
- Individualized, duty-linked assessment templates informed by applicable fair-chance law and counsel.
- Candidate document and licensing navigation.
- Job-readiness and manager coaching.
- Retention support and fast conflict escalation.
- Clear job success measures and 30/60/90-day plan.
- No public claim that a tax credit is available unless current law, worker eligibility, certification timing, and employer tax advice are confirmed. The IRS page reviewed in June 2026 still described WOTC authorization only through December 31, 2025, so Justice Grows should not market WOTC as a current incentive without updated authority.

## Demand economics

The coalition should initially be philanthropy- and sponsor-subsidized. Candidates never pay. Employers can later pay for implementation, training, workflow, and outcome reporting—not for access to conviction data.

A credible first-year employer team:

- Head of employer partnerships
- Two account executives/market leads
- Employer success and training lead
- Licensing/fair-chance operations counsel or specialist
- Two career navigators/retention coaches
- Data/impact analyst

## Pipeline math for 100 quality starts

Illustrative planning assumptions, not promised outcomes:

- 75 target accounts engaged
- 50 compact applications
- 35 verified employers
- 150 approved openings
- 500 candidate intakes
- 350 completed Passports
- 300 candidate-approved introductions
- 200 interviews
- 125 offers
- 100 starts
- 75 retained at 180 days

Each conversion must be measured by employer, role family, state, compensation, candidate preference, and reason for non-conversion.

## Employer accountability dashboard

Track publicly where cohort size and consent allow:

- Approved openings and median compensation
- Interview, offer, start, and retention conversion
- Time to decision
- Role withdrawal and employer no-show rate
- Percentage meeting candidate compensation floor
- Benefits and advancement path
- Candidate trust and safety score
- Complaint volume, resolution time, and status actions
- Promotion, wage growth, credential, and ownership outcomes

## First 90 days

### Days 1–30

- Recruit five design-partner employers and ten reentry/workforce partners
- Complete counsel-reviewed compact and hiring workflow
- Build target list of 150 cannabis, ancillary, and vendor employers
- Secure ten real role descriptions for review

### Days 31–60

- Train first hiring managers
- Approve at least 20 openings
- Complete 25 candidate Passports
- Run private matching and candidate feedback sessions
- Establish Federal Bonding and workforce-board referral contacts

### Days 61–90

- Produce first candidate-approved shortlists
- Track every disposition and delay
- Confirm first starts only after candidate and employer verification
- Publish employer status only when the evidence threshold is met
- Replace weak employers quickly; do not preserve logos at the expense of candidates
