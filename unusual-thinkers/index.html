A Right For All talent challenge

Help solve a problem that should not exist.

People in correctional settings are increasingly handed tablets, but the free layer is often too weak to teach, stabilize, or create real forward motion. A Right For All is working to make free GED prep and basic wellness the minimum standard on every correctional tablet. This challenge is how we find unusual thinkers who can help make that real.

What this mission actually needs

People who can hold human stakes, evidence discipline, product judgment, institutional reality, and speed at the same time.

What strong candidates usually notice

The first problem is rarely “build the full product.” The real work is choosing the right wedge, proving the truth, defining the thin serious version, and not wasting a year.

Where a strong fit can lead

Conversation, paid sprint, contract work, employee path, operator role, advisor role, or partner exploration. We care more about usefulness than title.

Mission brief

The operating context you would be stepping into

This challenge is grounded in the current shape of A Right For All. You do not need outside research to do this well. Strong answers come from reasoning clearly from the brief below and being honest about what you do not know.

Campaign ask

Free GED prep and basic wellness on every correctional tablet. This is the first minimum-rights standard, not the whole long-term vision.

Current wedge

The sharper issue is not “education never exists anywhere.” The sharper issue is that a reliable free learning-and-wellness baseline often does not meaningfully exist where the hardware rail already sits.

First map

Idaho first, with county jail and pre-prison idle time as the current emotional and strategic center.

Long-term product vision

A larger intelligent workbook supporting daily structure, regulation, learning, recovery, goal setting, reentry planning, and continuity across jail, prison, and release.

Year-one goal

Not “solve everything.” The real year-one goal is to become the most credible Idaho-first initiative pushing for a real free baseline on correctional tablets, with a believable path to pilot or partner traction.

Founder constraint

Founder time is limited. Early progress has to respect tight bandwidth, access constraints, and the need for a thin operating model that still creates real leverage.

Core message ladder: A tablet should be a bridge, not a paywall. The free section should teach. The rail exists. The right does not.
Verified facts you can treat as true for this challenge
  • Idaho prisons have both formal education infrastructure and facility-issued free tablets.
  • Khan Academy publicly states a free-education-for-anyone-anywhere mission and has published a correctional-facilities use case.
  • Free correctional access to Khan-based or Khan-style learning has existed in some other jurisdictions.
  • KA Lite is no longer the actively developed path; Kolibri is the current offline-first successor in that ecosystem.
  • The strongest public message is simple: the rail exists, but the right does not yet meaningfully exist.
Hard constraints and non-negotiables
  • Claims must stay evidence-backed.
  • Security, procurement, institutional trust, and correctional approval layers are real.
  • Some inside-facing work may require people who can clear institutional trust thresholds.
  • Early progress must be possible with limited founder time.
  • The first step should be thin, credible, and morally meaningful.
What not to optimize for too early
  • National optics before one real path.
  • Big product architecture before route selection.
  • Righteous anger without evidence discipline.
  • Celebrity or prestige outreach without leverage.
  • Generic awareness over a specific pilot, partner, or standard-setting route.
Open questions that still matter
  • What is the first real delivery path: partner, vendor, offline platform, thin custom layer, or policy-first?
  • What is the thinnest v1 that is still morally meaningful?
  • What exactly belongs in “basic wellness” for a correctional-tablet v1?
  • How should private clarification and public pressure be sequenced?
  • Who is the first high-trust operator or advisor needed?
Design intent: this is rich enough to reveal how you think. We do not need you to solve the entire mission. We care most about bottleneck judgment, truth discipline, and thin credible next steps.
Problem map

The hardest problems we think this mission actually turns on

This is our current map, not sacred truth. Part of the challenge is telling us where this map is wrong, incomplete, or mis-prioritized. The best candidates do not just admire the mission. They identify the actual bottleneck.

P1 · Reality map

Map what is actually true on the rail

What is free today, what is paid, where the strongest gaps are, what is degraded or missing, and who actually controls approvals.

  • Why hard: anecdotes, outdated examples, and official narratives do not automatically match the current user experience.
  • Bad move: making sweeping claims before the current-state map is solid.
P2 · Wedge selection

Choose the route that creates real leverage first

Quiet clarification, vendor path, offline partner, policy path, local pilot, or some hybrid sequence.

  • Why hard: the emotionally satisfying route is not always the fastest route to a real standard or pilot.
  • Bad move: trying to run every route at once.
P3 · Thin moral v1

Define the minimum meaningful free layer

What counts as real GED prep and basic wellness, what has to be included, what should wait, and what makes the free layer genuinely useful instead of token.

  • Why hard: the answer has to be both morally serious and operationally feasible.
  • Bad move: jumping straight to a giant AI product or, on the other side, settling for symbolic content.
P4 · Trust and access

Open serious doors in a system built to resist easy entry

Find the first real operator path, advisor path, or institutional conversation that can move the mission without wasting scarce founder time.

  • Why hard: corrections systems, vendors, educators, and reform groups all have different trust thresholds and incentives.
  • Bad move: assuming truth alone opens doors.
P5 · Rights and platform constraints

Navigate content rights, offline delivery, and integration reality

What content is licensable or usable, what needs permission, what can run offline, and what depends on vendor cooperation.

  • Why hard: “the content exists” is not the same as “the content can be deployed cleanly on this rail.”
  • Bad move: treating rights, security, or platform limits as details to solve later.
P6 · Incentives and economics

Make the free layer possible in a system not built around it

Understand where incentives align, where they conflict, and how a free baseline becomes hard to reject rather than merely nice to have.

  • Why hard: moral obviousness does not erase economics, vendor priorities, or bureaucratic inertia.
  • Bad move: assuming every actor wants the same outcome for the same reason.
P7 · Proof and measurement

Show enough value to create momentum

Define what evidence would make a pilot or partner path credible, what early metrics actually matter, and how to avoid fake precision.

  • Why hard: real-world evidence access may be thin, messy, or slow.
  • Bad move: waiting for perfect data or measuring only vanity metrics.
P8 · Story and escalation

Keep moral force without drifting into overclaiming or theater

Sequence founder story, evidence, private clarification, public pressure, and coalition-building so the mission becomes more credible as it gets louder.

  • Why hard: the project needs edge and discipline at the same time.
  • Bad move: overclaiming early or becoming so cautious that nothing moves.
The strongest candidates often do two things at once: they see how several of these problems connect, and then they still name the one that actually gates progress now.
How the filter works

A real challenge, not a vibe-based application page

We want signal, not theater. The process is designed to find people conventional screening often misses, without rewarding empty certainty or asking anyone to disclose a diagnosis.

What we score

Bottleneck judgment. Strategy under constraint. Evidence and assumption discipline. Quality of the spike you choose to show. Update behavior. Mission seriousness.

Accepted formats

Clean prose, bullets, diagrams described in text, structured outlines, spreadsheets, mockups, slides, or linked artifacts. Clarity matters more than essay style.

What gets filtered out

Unsupported claims, generic “build the full app” answers, inability to prioritize, prestige-chasing, defensive revision, and résumé theater disconnected from the mission.

What gets attention

  • You identify what the project should deliberately not do yet.
  • You propose the cheapest serious test before asking for a giant build.
  • You can hold human stakes and institutional constraints at the same time.
  • You treat evidence quality as part of the strategy, not an afterthought.

Fairness principles

  • Resume optional.
  • No hard timer.
  • Use of tools, including AI, is allowed if disclosed.
  • You never need to disclose a diagnosis to participate.
This is not free labor: the challenge is deliberately scoped around reasoning and bounded artifacts. Deeper mission work only happens in paid trials or paid roles.

FAQ

How to approach this well

Do I need a resume?

No. Proof beats pedigree. If you have a resume, you can include it later, but the challenge is designed so that you do not need one to be taken seriously.

Do I need outside research?

No. The mission brief is enough. You may use outside research if you want, but disclose it. We care more about judgment than volume.

Can I use AI?

Yes, if you disclose how. We care about reasoning, taste, and honesty. Undisclosed heavy tool use is a trust problem.

Can I answer in bullets or attach a different format?

Yes. Clean bullets, a memo, a spreadsheet, a mockup, a slide deck, a diagram, or a transcript can all work if they make your thinking clearer.

Is there a hard timer?

No. This is self-paced. We recommend doing it in one or two focused sessions instead of stretching it across weeks.

What usually makes a strong packet?

A clear bottleneck, disciplined assumptions, a thin credible plan, one real spike, clean revision behavior, and honest uncertainty where uncertainty is real.

Please do not include personal medical information, confidential legal details, or anything you would not want stored in a normal application process.
